Slots


ApplePi emulates the first 4 models of Apple II computers.  These models have seven expansion slotsnote1, numbered 1-7, which can hold peripheral controller cards.

When ApplePi is turned on, it (i.e. the internal ROM) will search for a disk controller in decreasing order beginning with slot 7, and boot from the first inserted disk image it finds.

Populated slots in the ApplePi:

Slot 1:    Printer card
Slot 3:    80-column card
Slot 6:    'Disk II' 5¼" floppy controller card
Slot 7:    A combination 3½" floppy / hard drive controller cardnote2

To use a slot, enter PR#n, (i.e, "print to slot n") , where 'n' is a number from 1-7.

Slot 1

See   Printing.


Slot 3

PR#3   switches the screen to an 80 column display. To switch back to a 40-column display, use  PR#0. <\p>

Slot 6

PR#6   boots from the 5¼" disk image in the Disk II drive labelled "Floppy 1". note3 <\p>

Slot 7

PR#7   boots from the 3½" floppy or hard disk image in the drive labelled "HD 1". note2 <\p>
<\p>



Note1:
There was also a special 'slot 0', called the 'language card slot' on the AppleII and II+, or the 'auxiliary slot' on the IIe.  This was physically and electronically incompatible with the expansion slots, and used for 80-column display circuitry and/or memory expansion.  Its existence in ApplePi is currently a combination of both.

Note 2:
(This type of controller may or may not have actually existed in the real world.  Just pretend, OK?)

Note 3:
The Disk II controller was an utterly brilliant design by Steve Wozniak which allowed the controller to be sold for far less than anything else on the market at that time, and small enough to fit inside the AppleII case.   An unavoidable side-effect of the design was a format completely incompatible with anything else on the planet.   So AppleII owners gained a lot (a disk drive they could afford), and lost only a little.   This is the reason it is not possible to share disk images between slots 6 and 7.
